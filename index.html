What Val should borrow

  • From David James / Keith Keating: business-first framing and refusal to be an order taker.
  • From Shannon Tipton: practical rebel energy, but attached to performance results.
  • From Philippa Hardman: evidence-forward confidence and clear ownership of a future-facing niche.
  • From Szilvia Olah: sharp pattern naming, but without becoming grievance-driven.
  • From April Dunford: positioning discipline: who it is for, what alternative it replaces, why it matters.

What Val should not copy

  • Do not copy anyone's persona, cadence, jokes, or provocation level.
  • Do not become anti-HR, anti-corporate, or performatively rebellious.
  • Do not move into pure AI futurism unless it serves transformation learning.
  • Do not sound like a vendor or platform evangelist.

Two ready posts from lesson 2

Post 1 · Go-live is not the finish line

Go-live is not the finish line. It is where the real adoption work starts.

Many transformation projects treat launch as proof of progress: the system is live, the training is complete, the communication has gone out.

But that is usually the moment when employees start discovering what the change actually means in their day-to-day work.

They run into exceptions. They create workarounds. Managers interpret the change differently. Some teams adapt quickly, while others quietly return to old habits.

That is not always resistance. Often, it is unclear work.

Smart teams do not stop at training completion. They watch what happens after launch: where people hesitate, where managers need support, where the process does not match reality, and where job aids or workflow support would do more than another meeting.

In transformation, adoption is not proven by a launch date. It is proven by new ways of working becoming normal.

Post 2 · More information is not more clarity

More information is not the same as more clarity.

This is one of the most common mistakes I see in complex change. When employees are confused, organizations often respond by adding more documentation, more training slides, more FAQs, more updates.

But people are rarely asking for more content. They are asking: "What do I need to do now?"

What decision do I make in this scenario? Who owns this step? What changed for my role? What does good look like now? Where do I go when the real case does not match the clean process map?

Clarity is not dumbing things down. Good clarity preserves the complexity that matters and removes the noise that blocks action.

Sometimes the answer is training. Sometimes it is a decision tree, a manager checklist, a job aid, a better workflow prompt, or a clearer story about why the change matters.

The goal is not to explain everything. The goal is to help people act with confidence when the work gets real.
